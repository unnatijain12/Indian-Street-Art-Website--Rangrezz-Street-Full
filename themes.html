THEMES IN INDIAN STREET ART

Home > Themes

Indian street art speaks about everything that matters. Here are the voices you will find most often on India's walls.

MYTHOLOGY & RELIGION

Indian street artists frequently draw from the country's vast mythological heritage. Scenes from the Ramayana and Mahabharata, depictions of Ganesha, Durga, Shiva, and countless other deities appear transformed into contemporary mural art on city walls. These works bridge the ancient and the modern — making timeless stories feel relevant and alive to urban audiences who encounter them on their daily commute.

The scale of street murals also gives mythology a new power — when Durga is ten metres tall on a Delhi wall, she commands attention in a way no temple image can.

BOLLYWOOD & POP CULTURE

Bollywood is India's most dominant cultural force and its influence on street art is massive. Giant portrait murals of film icons from Raj Kapoor and Guru Dutt to Ranveer Singh and Deepika Padukone appear on walls across the country.

But Indian street art also critiques Bollywood — questioning its portrayal of women, its manufactured dreams, and its erasure of regional identities. This tension between celebration and critique makes Bollywood-themed street art some of the most intellectually interesting work being done on Indian walls today.

WOMEN EMPOWERMENT

Some of India's most powerful and emotionally affecting street art addresses gender equality, women's safety, and female identity. After the 2012 Delhi gang rape case, murals expressing outrage and solidarity appeared across the country in a spontaneous national response.

Artists like Guesswho regularly paint powerful female figures — not passive or decorative but strong, complex, and fully present. The walls of Indian cities have become a feminist space where women's stories are told at the largest possible scale and in the most public possible places.

ENVIRONMENT & NATURE

As India faces severe and worsening environmental challenges — air pollution in its cities, water scarcity across its plains, deforestation in its hills — its street artists are responding with both grief and anger.

Murals of endangered animals, dying rivers, polluted skylines, and melting glaciers appear alongside celebrations of India's extraordinary natural beauty. Environmental street art in India is simultaneously mourning and activism — grieving what is being lost while demanding loudly that something be done about it.

SOCIAL JUSTICE & COMMUNITY

India's long and unresolved struggle with caste discrimination, communal tension, and economic inequality finds direct expression in its street art. Ambedkarite artists paint tributes to Dr. B.R. Ambedkar and scenes of Dalit resistance and dignity.

Other artists tackle communal harmony, linguistic identity, the rights of tribal communities, and the stories of those who are made invisible by mainstream society. Street art in India is one of the few spaces where the most difficult social conversations happen openly, publicly, and without a paywall.
