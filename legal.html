LEGAL STREET ART IN INDIA

Home > Legal

Art With Permission

Legal street art refers to murals and artwork created in public spaces with the explicit permission of the building owner, municipal government, or relevant authority. In India, legal street art has grown dramatically over the past decade, driven by government beautification programmes, corporate sponsorship, and most importantly by nonprofit organisations like St+art India Foundation.

Legal murals are different from illegal graffiti in important ways. They are planned in advance, the artist is usually paid or supported with materials, the work is maintained and preserved, and it contributes positively to the official cultural landscape of the city.

The existence of a robust legal street art scene in India has elevated the entire art form — giving talented artists opportunities to work at large scale without risk, and giving Indian cities some of their most celebrated landmarks.

Government Initiatives

Lodhi Art District, Delhi

Created in 2014–15 through a partnership between St+art India Foundation and the Municipal Corporation of Delhi. Over 50 murals on government colony walls by Indian and international artists.

Now one of Delhi's most visited destinations and a protected cultural site. Entry is free and open to the public.

Sassoon Docks Art Project, Mumbai

Mumbai Port Trust partnered with St+art India Foundation to transform the historic 19th century Sassoon Docks into an art destination.

First launched in 2018 and expanded annually, covering working dock buildings and boats with large-scale murals.

Smart Cities Mission

The Indian government's Smart Cities Mission has funded public art projects in over 50 cities as part of beautification initiatives.

Hundreds of murals across tier 2 and tier 3 cities have emerged under this scheme.

Metro Rail Beautification

Delhi Metro, Mumbai Metro, Bengaluru Metro and other city systems have commissioned murals on station walls, pillars and infrastructure.

Art now reaches millions of daily commuters.

St+art India Foundation

Founded in 2013, St+art India is a nonprofit that built the infrastructure for legal public art in India.

They organise festivals, partner with governments and corporates, and have enabled hundreds of Indian artists to work legally at scale.

Where Is the Line?

The difference between celebrated street art and criminal vandalism is often simply permission — and yet the line is not always as clear as it seems. Here is how to understand the distinction:

Legal Street Art

  • Created with explicit written permission
  • Planned and approved in advance
  • Artist identified and compensated
  • Maintained and preserved over time
  • Enhances neighbourhood character
  • Featured in tourism materials
  • No legal consequences

Illegal Graffiti

  • Created without permission
  • Spontaneous location and content
  • Artist anonymous
  • Removed or painted over
  • Prosecutable under Prevention of Damage to Public Property Act, 1984
  • Subject to fines or imprisonment
  • Despite illegality, has produced important art
